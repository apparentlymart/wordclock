- fünf nach halb drei -- five after half to three (twenty five to three)

A word clock using Latin words, maybe?
The romans apparently defined "hours" as 12 subdivisions between sunrise and
sunset, and so the lengths of their hours varied throughout the year and
didn't line up with ours.
However, they had some vocabulary for time-telling that we can borrow and
pervert to describe our modern timekeeping system.

This doesn't work because all of the words end with A but none of
them begin with A, and so we don't benefit from any "compression" achieved
by running words together.

I don't know Russian at all, so this is probably very wrong.
But I just wanted to try a non-Latin language and see how that fits.
Not possible to do properly in russian because ОДИ́ННАДЦАТЬ (11)
is 11 characters long. So Wedged the ТЬ together into a single cell.
Also takes 12 rows to fit everything in, because in Russian they
(apparently) count minutes all the way up to the 50s, rather than
switching to "minutes until next hour" at half past.

1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8 9 0
С Е М Ь П Я Т Ь        (hour, 7, 5)
Д В Е Н А́ Д Ц А Т Ь    (12)
О Д И́ Н Н А Д Ц А ТЬ   (11, with ТЬ together in one cell because this word is too long)
Ч Е Т Ы́ Р Е Д В А      (4, 2)
  В О́ С Е М Ь Т Р И    (8, 3)
Ч А С О́ В С О́ Р О К    ("hour"/"hours", 40 - words run together with no space so it'll fit)
П Я Т Н А́ Д Ц А Т Ь    (15)
Д Е́ С Я Т Ь            (10)
Д В А́ Д Ц А Т Ь        (20)
    Т Р И́ Д Ц А Т Ь    (30)
П Я Т Ь Д Е С Я́ Т      (50)
П Я Т Ь                (5)
